Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are some of the most fundamental concepts in linear algebra. They tell us about the core characteristics of linear transformations.

## The Intuition

Given a square matrix $A$, an **eigenvector** $v$ is a non-zero vector whose direction remains unchanged when the linear transformation $A$ is applied to it. Instead of changing direction, it is simply scaled by a scalar value $\lambda$, which is called the **eigenvalue**.

This relationship is perfectly captured by the most elegant equation in linear algebra:

$$
A \mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}
$$

### Finding Eigenvalues

To find the eigenvalues, we rewrite the equation as:

$$
(A - \lambda I) \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}
$$

For a non-zero vector $\mathbf{v}$ to exist, the matrix $(A - \lambda I)$ must be singular (non-invertible). This means its determinant must be zero:

$$
\det(A - \lambda I) = 0
$$

This is known as the **characteristic equation**.

### Example

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

First, we set up the characteristic equation:

$$
\det \left( \begin{bmatrix} 2 - \lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 2 - \lambda \end{bmatrix} \right) = (2 - \lambda)^2 - 1 = 0
$$

Expanding this gives:

$$
\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0
$$
$$
(\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 1) = 0
$$

So the eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 3$ and $\lambda_2 = 1$.
